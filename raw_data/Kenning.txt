        A kenning  is a type of  |circumlocution|Circumlocution| , in the form of a  |compound|Compound|  that employs figurative language in place of a more concrete single-word  |noun|Noun| . Kennings are strongly associated with  |Old_Norse|Old_Norse_Poetry|  and later  |Icelandic|Icelandic_Literatureskaldic_Poetry|  and  |Old_English|Old_English_Literaturepoetry|  poetry.     They usually consist of two words, and are often hyphenated. For example, Old Norse poets might replace sverð  |sword|Sword|  with an abstract compound such as wound-hoe  or a  |genitive|Genitive| |phrase|Phrase|  such as randa íss ice of shields . Modern scholars have also applied the term kenning to similar  |figures_of_speech|Figure_Of_Speech|  in other languages, especially  |Old_English|Old_English| .       The word was adopted into English in the nineteenth century The Oxford English Dictionary says late 19th century . from medieval Icelandic treatises on poetics, in particular the  |Prose_Edda|Prose_Edda|  of  |Snorri_Sturluson|Snorri_Sturluson| , and derives ultimately from the Old Norse verb kenna know, recognise; perceive, feel; show; teach , etc., as used in the expression kenna við to name after; to express in terms of ,  name after; refer to in terms of , Faulkes, Anthony . and kenna til qualify by, make into a kenning by adding .     The corresponding modern verb to ken survives only in Scots and highly remote English dialects, other than the derivative existing in the standard language in the set expression beyond ones ken, beyond the scope of ones knowledge and in the phonologically altered forms uncanny, surreal or supernatural , and canny, shrewd , prudent .
