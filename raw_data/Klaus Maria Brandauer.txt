     Klaus Maria Brandauer  is an  |Austrian|Austrians|  actor and director. He is also a  |professor|Professor|  at the  |Max_Reinhardt_Seminar|Max_Reinhardt_Seminar| . Brandauer is known internationally for his roles in  Mephisto  ,  Never Say Never Again  ,  Out of Africa  ,  Hanussen  ,  Burning Secret  ,  Introducing Dorothy Dandridge  , and  The Strange Case of Wilhelm Reich  .     For his supporting role as  |Bror_von_Blixen_Finecke|Bror_Von_Blixen_Finecke|  in the drama film  Out of Africa  , Brandauer was nominated for an  |Academy_Award|Academy_Award_For_Best_Supporting_Actor|  and won a  |Golden_Globe_Award|Golden_Globe_Award_For_Best_Supporting_Actor_–_Motion_Picture| .       Brandauer was born as Klaus Georg Steng in  |Bad_Aussee|Bad_Aussee| ,  |Austria|Austria| .  He is the son of Maria Brandauer and Georg Steng , a civil servant.  He subsequently took his mothers first name as part of his professional name, Klaus Maria Brandauer.       Brandauer began acting on stage in 1962. After working in national theatre and television, he made his film debut in English in 1972, in  The Salzburg Connection . In 1975 he played in  Derrick  – in Season 2, Episode 8 called Pfandhaus . His starring and award-winning role in  |István_Szabós|István_Szabó|   Mephisto  playing a self-absorbed actor, launched his international career.     Following his role in Mephisto, Brandauer appeared as  |Maximillian_Largo|Maximillian_Largo|  in  Never Say Never Again  , a remake of the 1965 James Bond film  Thunderball .  |Roger_Ebert|Roger_Ebert|  said of his performance: For one thing, theres more of a human element in the movie, and it comes from Klaus Maria Brandauer, as Largo. Brandauer is a wonderful actor, and he chooses not to play the villain as a cliché. Instead, he brings a certain poignancy and charm to Largo, and since Connery always has been a particularly human James Bond, the emotional stakes are more convincing this time.  He starred in  Out of Africa  , opposite  |Meryl_Streep|Meryl_Streep|  and  |Robert_Redford|Robert_Redford| , for which he was nominated for an  |Oscar|Academy_Awards|  and won a  |Golden_Globe|Golden_Globe| , and Szabós  Oberst Redl  .     In 1987, he was the Head of the Jury at the  |37th_Berlin_International_Film_Festival|37Th_Berlin_International_Film_Festival| .      In 1988 he appeared in  Hanussen  opposite  |Erland_Josephson|Erland_Josephson|  and  |Ildikó_Bánsági|Ildikó_Bánsági| . Brandauer was originally cast as Marko Ramius in  The Hunt for Red October . That role eventually went to  |Oscar|Academy_Awards|  nominee  |Sean_Connery|Sean_Connery| , who played  |James_Bond|James_Bond|  to Brandauers Largo in  Never Say Never Again  . He co-starred with Connery again in  The Russia House  . His other film roles have been in  The Lightship  ,  Streets of Gold  ,  Burning Secret  ,  The Russia House  ,  White Fang  , Becoming Colette ,  Introducing Dorothy Dandridge  , and  Everymans Feast  . In 1989 he participated in the great production film for the bicentennial of the French Revolution by the French television channel TF1,  La Révolution française  |Georges_Danton|Georges_Danton| .     Brandauer first work as movie director was, in 1989, , with himself in the title role.     In August 2006, Brandauers much-awaited production of  The Threepenny Opera  gained a mixed reception. Brandauer had resisted questions about how his production of  |Bertolt_Brecht|Bertolt_Brecht|  and  |Kurt_Weills|Kurt_Weill|  classic musical comedy about the criminal MacHeath would differ from earlier versions, and his production featured Mack the Knife in a three-piece suit and white gloves, stuck to Brechts text, and avoided any references to contemporary politics or issues.     Brandauer has at least a working knowledge of five languages:  |German|German_Language| ,  |Italian|Italian_Language| ,  |Hungarian|Hungarian_Language| ,  |English|English_Language|  and  |French|French_Language|  and has acted in each.       His first wife was Karin Katharina Müller , an Austrian film and television director and screenwriter, from 1963 until her death in 1992, aged 47, from cancer. Both were teenagers when they married, in 1963. They had one son, Christian.
