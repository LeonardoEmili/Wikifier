              Alexander Fyodorovich Kerensky  was a Russian lawyer and revolutionist who was a key political figure in the  |Russian_Revolution|Russian_Revolution|  of 1917. After the  |February_Revolution_of_1917|February_Revolution| , he joined the newly formed  |Russian_Provisional_Government|Russian_Provisional_Government| , first as  |Minister_of_Justice|Justice_Ministry| , then as  |Minister_of_War|Minister_Of_War| , and after July as the governments  |second|List_Of_Heads_Of_Government_Of_Russiarussian_Provisional_Republic| |Minister-Chairman|Prime_Minister_Of_Russia| . A leader of the moderate-socialist  |Trudovik|Trudovik|  faction of the  |Socialist_Revolutionary_Party|Socialist_Revolutionary_Party| , he was also vice-chairman of the powerful  |Petrograd_Soviet|Petrograd_Soviet| . On 7 November, his government was overthrown by the  |Lenin|Vladimir_Lenin| -led  |Bolsheviks|Bolshevik|  in the  |October_Revolution|October_Revolution| . He spent the remainder of his life in exile, in Paris and New York City, and worked for the  |Hoover_Institution|Hoover_Institution| .       Alexander Kerensky was born in Simbirsk  on the  |Volga_River|Volga_River|  on 4 May 1881 and was the eldest son in the family.  His father, Fyodor Mikhailovich Kerensky, was a teacher and director of the local  |gymnasium|Gymnasium|  and was later promoted to Inspector of public schools. His maternal grandfather was head of the Topographical Bureau of the  |Kazan|Kazan| |Military_District|Military_District| . His mother, Nadezhda Aleksandrovna ,  was the granddaughter of a former  |serf|Serf|  who had managed to purchase his freedom before  |serfdom_was_abolished_in_1861|Emancipation_Reform_Of_1861| . He subsequently embarked upon a mercantile career, in which he prospered. This allowed him to move his business to Moscow, where he continued his success, and became a wealthy Moscow merchant.       Kerenskys father was the teacher of  |Vladimir_Ulyanov|Vladimir_Lenin| , and members of the Kerensky and Ulyanov families were friends. In 1889, when Kerensky was eight, the family moved to  |Tashkent|Tashkent| , where his father had been appointed the main inspector of public schools . Alexander graduated with honours in 1899. The same year he entered  |St._Petersburg_University|St._Petersburg_University| , where he studied history and  |philology|Philology| . The next year he switched to law. He earned his law degree in 1904 and married Olga Lvovna Baranovskaya, the daughter of a Russian general, the same year. At the end of 1904, he was jailed on suspicion of belonging to a militant group. Afterwards he gained a reputation for his work as a defence lawyer in a number of political trials of revolutionaries. In 1912, Kerensky became widely known when he visited the goldfields at the  |Lena_River|Lena_River|  and published material about the  |Lena_Minefields_incident|Lena_Minefields_Incident| .
