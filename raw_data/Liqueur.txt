     A liqueur  is an  |alcoholic_drink|Alcoholic_Drink|  composed of  |distilled_spirits|Liquor|  and additional flavorings such as  |sugar|Sugar| ,  |fruits|Fruit| ,  |herbs|Herb| , and  |spices|Spice| . Often served with or after  |dessert|Dessert| , they are typically heavily sweetened and un-aged beyond a resting period during production, when necessary, for their flavors to mingle.     Liqueurs are historical descendants of  |herbal_medicines|Herbal_Medicine| . They were made in Italy as early as the 13th century, often prepared by  |monks|Monk|  . Today they are produced the world over, commonly served straight, over ice, with coffee, in  |cocktails|Cocktails| , and used in  |cooking|Cooking| .     In some areas of the United States and Canada liqueurs are also referred to as cordials or  schnapps , though the terms refer to different beverages elsewhere.       The French word liqueur is derived from the Latin liquifacere, which means to dissolve . In the United States and Canada, where spirits are often called liquor , there is often confusion discerning between liqueurs and liquors, due to the many different types of flavored spirits that are available today . Liqueurs generally contain a lower  |alcohol|Ethanol|  content  than spirits  and it has sweetener mixed, while some can have an ABV as high as 55%.        Under the Food and Drug Regulations , liqueurs are produced from mixing alcohol with plant materials. These materials include juices or extracts from fruits, flowers, leaves or other plant materials. The extracts are obtained by soaking, filtering or softening the plant substances. A sweetening agent should be added in an amount that is at least 2.5 percent of the finished liqueur. The alcohol percentage shall be at least 23%. It may also contain natural or artificial flavouring and color.        The  |Alcohol_and_Tobacco_Tax_and_Trade_Bureau|Alcohol_And_Tobacco_Tax_And_Trade_Bureau|  regulates liqueurs similarly to Canada, requiring that alcohol be mixed with plant products and sweeteners be added to at least 2.5% by weight.
