Information theory studies the transmission, processing, extraction, and utilization of information. Abstractly, information can be thought of as the resolution of uncertainty. In the case of communication of information over a noisy channel, this abstract concept was made concrete in 1948 by  |Claude_Shannon|Claude_Shannon|  in his paper  |A_Mathematical_Theory_of_Communication|A_Mathematical_Theory_Of_Communication|  , in which information is thought of as a set of possible messages, where the goal is to send these messages over a noisy channel, and then to have the receiver reconstruct the message with low probability of error, in spite of the channel noise. Shannons main result, the  |noisy_channel_coding_theorem|Noisy_Channel_Coding_Theorem|  showed that, in the limit of many channel uses, the rate of information that is asymptotically achievable is equal to the  |channel_capacity|Channel_Capacity| , a quantity dependent merely on the statistics of the channel over which the messages are sent.     Information theory is closely associated with a collection of pure and applied disciplines that have been investigated and reduced to engineering practice under a variety of  |rubrics|Rubric|  throughout the world over the past half century or more:  |adaptive_systems|Adaptive_System| ,  |anticipatory_systems|Anticipatory_System| ,  |artificial_intelligence|Artificial_Intelligence| ,  |complex_systems|Complex_System| ,  |complexity_science|Complexity_Science| ,  |cybernetics|Cybernetics| ,  |informatics|Informatics| ,  |machine_learning|Machine_Learning| , along with  |systems_sciences|Systems_Science|  of many descriptions. Information theory is a broad and deep mathematical theory, with equally broad and deep applications, amongst which is the vital field of  |coding_theory|Coding_Theory| .     Coding theory is concerned with finding explicit methods, called codes, for increasing the efficiency and reducing the error rate of data communication over noisy channels to near the  |channel_capacity|Channel_Capacity| . These codes can be roughly subdivided into  |data_compression|Data_Compression|  and  |error_correction|Error_Correction|  techniques. In the latter case, it took many years to find the methods Shannons work proved were possible.     A third class of information theory codes are cryptographic algorithms . Concepts, methods and results from coding theory and information theory are widely used in  |cryptography|Cryptography|  and  |cryptanalysis|Cryptanalysis| . Information theory is based on  |probability_theory|Probability_Theory|  and  |statistics|Statistics| . Information theory often concerns itself with measures of information of the distributions associated with random variables. Important quantities of information are  |entropy|Entropy| , a measure of information in a single  |random_variable|Random_Variable| , and  |mutual_information|Mutual_Information| , a measure of information in common between two random variables. The former quantity is a property of the probability distribution of a random variable and gives a limit on the rate at which data generated by independent samples with the given distribution can be reliably  |compressed|Data_Compression| . The latter is a property of the joint distribution of two random variables, and is the maximum rate of reliable communication across a noisy  |channel|Communication_Channel|  in the limit of long block lengths, when the channel statistics are determined by the joint distribution. Information theory leads us to believe it is much more difficult to keep secrets than it might first appear. A  |brute_force_attack|Brute_Force_Attack|  can break systems based on  |asymmetric_key_algorithms|Public_Key_Cryptography|  or on most commonly used methods of  |symmetric_key_algorithms|Symmetric_Key_Algorithm|  , such as  |block_ciphers|Block_Cipher| . The security of all such methods currently comes from the assumption that no known attack can break them in a practical amount of time.      |Information_theoretic_security|Information_Theoretic_Security|  refers to methods such as the  |one_time_pad|One_Time_Pad|  that are not vulnerable to such brute force attacks. In such cases, the positive conditional  |mutual_information|Mutual_Information|  between the  |plaintext|Plaintext|  and  |ciphertext|Ciphertext|   can ensure proper transmission, while the unconditional mutual information between the plaintext and ciphertext remains zero, resulting in absolutely secure communications. In other words, an eavesdropper would not be able to improve his or her guess of the plaintext by gaining knowledge of the ciphertext but not of the key.
