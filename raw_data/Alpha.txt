The letter alpha represents various concepts in  |physics|Physics|  and  |chemistry|Chemistry| , including  |alpha_radiation|Alpha_Radiation| ,  |angular_acceleration|Angular_Acceleration| ,  |alpha_particles|Alpha_Particle| ,  |alpha_carbon|Alpha_Carbon|  and strength of  |electromagnetic_interaction|Electromagnetic_Interaction|  . Alpha also stands for  |thermal_expansion_coefficient|Thermal_Expansion_Coefficient|  of a  |compound|Chemical_Compound|  in  |physical_chemistry|Physical_Chemistry| . It is also commonly used in  |mathematics|Mathematics|  in  |algebraic_solutions|Algebra|  representing quantities such as angles. Furthermore, in mathematics, the letter alpha is used to denote the area underneath a  |normal_curve|Normal_Distribution|  in  |statistics|Statistics|  to denote  |significance_level|Statistical_Significance|   when proving  |null|Null_Hypotheses|  and  |alternative_hypotheses|Alternative_Hypothesis| . In zoology, it is used to name the dominant individual in a wolf or dog pack. In aerodynamics, the letter is used as a symbol for the  |angle_of_attack|Angle_Of_Attack|  of an aircraft and the word alpha is used as a synonym for this property.     The proportionality operator  |∝|∝|   is sometimes mistaken for alpha.     The uppercase letter alpha is not generally used as a symbol because it tends to be rendered identically to the uppercase  |Latin_A|A| .       In the  |International_Phonetic_Alphabet|International_Phonetic_Alphabet| , the letter ɑ, which looks similar to the lower-case alpha, represents the  |open_back_unrounded_vowel|Open_Back_Unrounded_Vowel| .           Alpha was derived from  aleph , which in  |Phoenician|Phoenician_Language|  means  |ox|Ox|  .  on the Online Etymology Dictionary        |Plutarch|Plutarch| , in  Moralia , Symposiacs, Book IX, questions II & III   at Adelaide library presents a discussion on why the letter alpha stands first in the alphabet. Ammonius asks Plutarch what he, being a  |Boeotia|Boeotia| n, has to say for  |Cadmus|Cadmus| , the  |Phoenicia|Phoenicia| n who reputedly settled in  |Thebes|Thebes|  and introduced the alphabet to Greece, placing alpha first because it is the Phoenician name for  |ox|Ox| —which, unlike  |Hesiod|Hesiod| , Hesiod, in  Works and Days  , advises the early Greek farmers, First of all, get a house, then a woman and third, an ox for the plough. the Phoenicians considered not the second or third, but the first of all necessities. Nothing at all, Plutarch replied. He then added that he would rather be assisted by  |Lamprias|Lamprias| , his own grandfather, than by  |Dionysus|Dionysus|  grandfather, i.e. Cadmus. For Lamprias had said that the first articulate sound made is alpha , because it is very plain and simple—the air coming off the mouth does not require any motion of the tongue—and therefore this is the first sound that children make.     According to Plutarchs natural order of attribution of the  |vowels|Vowel|  to the  |planets|Planet| , alpha was connected with the  |Moon|Moon| .          Alpha, both as a symbol and term, is used to refer to or describe a variety of things, including the first or most significant occurrence of something. The New Testament has God declaring himself to be the  |Alpha_and_Omega|Alpha_And_Omega| , the beginning and the end, the first and the last. .
