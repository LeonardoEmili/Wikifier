        Chervil , sometimes called French parsley or garden chervil , is a delicate  |annual|Annual_Plant| |herb|Herb|  related to  |parsley|Parsley| . It is commonly used to season mild-flavoured dishes and is a constituent of the French herb mixture .         The name chervil is from  |Anglo-Norman|Anglo-Norman_Language| , from  |Latin|Latin|   or , ultimately from  |Ancient_Greek|Ancient_Greek|   , meaning leaves of joy .         A member of the  |Apiaceae|Apiaceae| , chervil is native to the  |Caucasus|Caucasus|  but was spread by the Romans through most of Europe, where it is now naturalised.  It is also grown frequently in the United States, where it sometimes escapes cultivation. Such escape can be recognized, however, as garden chervil is distinguished from all other Anthriscus species growing in North America by its having lanceolate-linear bracteoles and a fruit with a relatively long beak. Chervil is used, particularly in France, to season  |poultry|Poultry| ,  |seafood|Seafood| , young spring  |vegetables|Vegetable|  , soups, and sauces. More delicate than parsley, it has a faint taste of  |liquorice|Liquorice|  or  |aniseed|Aniseed| . According to some,  |slugs|Slug|  are attracted to chervil and the plant is sometimes used to bait them.        Chervil has had various uses in folk medicine. It was claimed to be useful as a  |digestive|Digestion|  aid, for lowering  |high_blood_pressure|High_Blood_Pressure| , and, infused with  |vinegar|Vinegar| , for curing  |hiccups|Hiccups| .  Besides its digestive properties, it is used as a mild stimulant.     Chervil has also been implicated in strimmer  |dermatitis|Dermatitis|  , another name for  |phytophotodermatitis|Phytophotodermatitis| , due to spray from weed trimmers and similar forms of contact. Other plants in the family  |Apiaceae|Apiaceae|  can have similar effects.        Transplanting chervil can be difficult, due to the long  |taproot|Taproot| . It prefers a cool and moist location; otherwise, it rapidly goes to seed . It is usually grown as a cool-season crop, like  |lettuce|Lettuce| , and should be planted in early spring and late fall or in a winter greenhouse. Regular harvesting of leaves also helps to prevent bolting. If plants bolt despite precautions, the plant can be periodically re-sown throughout the growing season, thus producing fresh plants as older plants bolt and go out of production.
