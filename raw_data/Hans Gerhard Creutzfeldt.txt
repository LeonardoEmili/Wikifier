Hans Gerhard Creutzfeldt was a  |German|Germany| |neurologist|Neurologist|  and  |neuropathologist|Neuropathologist| . Although he is typically credited as the physician to first describe the  |Creutzfeldt–Jakob_disease|Creutzfeldt–Jakob_Disease| , this has been disputed. He was born in  |Harburg_upon_Elbe|Harburg|  and died in  |Munich|Munich| .       Hans Gerhard Creutzfeldt was born into a medical family in  |Harburg|Harburg| , which was incorporated into  |Hamburg|Hamburg|  in 1937. In 1903, at the age of 18, he was drafted into the German army and spent his service stationed in Kiel. Afterwards, he attended the School of Medicine of the Universities of  |University_of_Jena|University_Of_Jena|  and  |University_of_Rostock|University_Of_Rostock| , receiving his doctorate at the latter in 1909. Part of his practical training was undertaken at St. Georg – Hospital in Hamburg. After qualification he sought adventure as a ships surgeon, voyaging the  |Pacific_Ocean|Pacific_Ocean| , taking the opportunity to study local crafts, linguistics, and tropical plants.     After returning to  |Germany|Germany| , Creutzfeldt worked at the Neurological Institute in  |Frankfurt_am_Main|Frankfurt_Am_Main| , at the psychiatric-neurological clinics in  |Breslau|Wrocław| ,  |Kiel|Kiel|  and  |Berlin|Berlin| , and at the  Deutsche Forschungsanstalt für Psychiatrie  in  |Munich|Munich| . He was  |habilitated|Habilitation|  at Kiel in 1920, and in 1925 became Extraordinarius of  |psychiatry|Psychiatry|  and  |neurology|Neurology| . In 1938 he was appointed professor and director of the university psychiatric and neurological division in Kiel. He helped to recognize a neurodegenerative disease, with  |Alfons_Maria_Jakob|Alfons_Maria_Jakob| ,  |Creutzfeldt–Jakob_disease|Creutzfeldt–Jakob_Disease|  in which the  |brain_tissue|Brain_Tissue|  develops holes and takes on a sponge-like texture. It is now known it is due to a type of infectious  |protein|Protein|  called a  |prion|Prion| . Prions are misfolded proteins which replicate by converting their properly folded counterparts.     In the  |Third_Reich|Third_Reich| , Creutzfeldt became a  |Patron_Member|Förderndes_Mitglied_Der_Ss|  of  |Heinrich_Himmlers|Heinrich_Himmler| |SS|Schutzstaffel|  from 1932 to 1933.       Creutzfeldt was 54 years old when the  |Second_World_War|Second_World_War|  broke out. He was unmoved by the  |Nazi_regime|Nazi_Regime|  and was able to save some people from death in  |concentration_camps|Extermination_Camp|  and also managed to rescue almost all of his patients from being murdered under the Nazi  |Aktion_T4|Aktion_T4| |euthanasia|Euthanasia|  program, an unusual event since most mental patients identified by T4 personnel were gassed or poisoned at separate  |euthanasia|Euthanasia|  clinics such as  |Hadamar_Euthanasia_Centre|Hadamar_Euthanasia_Centre| . During the war, bombing raids destroyed his home and clinic.     After the war he was director of the  |University_of_Kiel|University_Of_Kiel|  for six months, before being dismissed by the British occupation forces. His efforts to rebuild the university caused a series of conflicts with the British because he wanted to allow more former army officers to study there. In 1953 he moved on to  |Munich|Munich|  to work on scientific research commissioned by the  |Max_Planck_Society|Max_Planck_Society| .       He was married to Clara Sombart, a daughter of  |Werner_Sombart|Werner_Sombart| . They had five children, among them  |Otto_Detlev_Creutzfeldt|Otto_Detlev_Creutzfeldt|  and  |Werner_Creutzfeldt|Werner_Creutzfeldt|  , a renowned German  |Internist|Internist| .
