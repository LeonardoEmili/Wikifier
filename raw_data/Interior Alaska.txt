      Interior Alaska is the central region of  |Alaskas|Alaska|  territory, roughly bounded by the  |Alaska_Range|Alaska_Range|  to the south and the  |Brooks_Range|Brooks_Range|  to the north. It is largely  |wilderness|Wilderness| . Mountains include  |Denali|Denali|  in the  |Alaska_Range|Alaska_Range| , the  |Wrangell_Mountains|Wrangell_Mountains| , and the  |Ray_Mountains|Ray_Mountains| . The  |native_people|Alaska_Natives|  of the interior are  |Alaskan_Athabaskans|Alaskan_Athabaskans| . The largest city in the interior is  |Fairbanks|Fairbanks,_Alaska| ,  |Alaskas|Alaska|  second-largest city, in the  |Tanana_Valley|Tanana_Valley| . Other towns include  |North_Pole|North_Pole,_Alaska| , just southeast of Fairbanks,  |Eagle|Eagle,_Alaska| ,  |Tok|Tok,_Alaska| ,  |Glennallen|Glennallen,_Alaska| ,  |Delta_Junction|Delta_Junction,_Alaska| ,  |Nenana|Nenana,_Alaska| ,  |Anderson|Anderson,_Alaska| ,  |Healy|Healy,_Alaska|  and  |Cantwell|Cantwell,_Alaska| . The interior region has an estimated population of 113,154.     TOC            Interior Alaska experiences extreme seasonal temperature variability. Winter temperatures in Fairbanks average −12  ° |F|Fahrenheit|   and summer temperatures average +62  °F . Temperatures there have been recorded as low as −65  °F in mid-winter, and as high as +99  °F in summer. Both the highest and lowest temperature records for the state were set in the Interior, with 100  °F in  |Fort_Yukon|Fort_Yukon,_Alaska|  and −80  °F in  |Prospect_Creek|Prospect_Creek,_Alaska| .  Temperatures within a given winter are highly variable as well; extended cold snaps of forty below zero can be followed by unseasonable warmth with temperatures above freezing due to  |chinook_wind|Chinook_Wind|  effects.     Summers can be warm and dry for extended periods creating ideal fire weather conditions. Weak thunderstorms produce mostly dry lightning, sparking wildfires that are mostly left to burn themselves out as they are often far from populated areas. The  |2004_season|2004_Alaska_Fire_Season|  set a new record with over  burned.      The average annual precipitation in Fairbanks is 11.3  inches . Most of this comes in the form of snow during the winter. Most storms in the interior of Alaska originate in the  |Gulf_of_Alaska|Gulf_Of_Alaska| , south of the state, though these storms often have limited precipitation due to a  |rain_shadow|Rain_Shadow|  effect caused by the  |Alaska_Range|Alaska_Range| .     On clear winter nights, the  |aurora_borealis|Aurora_Borealis|  can often be seen dancing in the sky. Like all subarctic regions, the months from May to July in the summer have no night, only a twilight during the night hours. The months of November to January have little daylight. Fairbanks receives an average 21 hours of daylight between May 10 and August 2 each summer, and an average of less than four hours of daylight between November 18 and January 24 each winter.     The interior of Alaska is largely underlined by discontinuous  |permafrost|Permafrost| , which grades to continuous permafrost as the  |Arctic_Circle|Arctic_Circle|  is approached.
