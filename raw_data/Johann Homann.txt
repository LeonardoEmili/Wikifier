   Johann Baptist Homann was a  |German|Germany| |geographer|Geographer|  and  |cartographer|Cartography| , who also made maps of the  |Americas|Americas| .       Homann was born in Oberkammlach near  |Kammlach|Kammlach|  in the  |Electorate_of_Bavaria|Electorate_Of_Bavaria| . Although educated at a  |Jesuit|Society_Of_Jesus|  school, and preparing for an ecclesiastical career, he eventually converted to  |Protestantism|Protestantism|  and from 1687 worked as a  |civil_law_notary|Civil_Law_Notary|  in  |Nuremberg|Free_Imperial_City_Of_Nuremberg| . He soon turned to  |engraving|Engraving|  and cartography; in 1702 he founded his own publishing house.     Homann acquired renown as a leading German cartographer, and in 1715 was appointed Imperial Geographer by Emperor  |Charles_VI|Charles_Vi,_Holy_Roman_Emperor| . Giving such privileges to individuals was an added right that the  |Holy_Roman_Emperor|Holy_Roman_Emperor|  enjoyed. In the same year he was also named a member of the  |Prussian_Academy_of_Sciences|Prussian_Academy_Of_Sciences|  in  |Berlin|Berlin| . Of particular significance to cartography were the imperial  |printing_privileges|Printing_Patent|  . These protected for a time the authors in all scientific fields such as printers,  |copper|Copper|  engravers, map makers and publishers. They were also very important as a recommendation for potential customers.     In 1716 Homann published his masterpiece Grosser Atlas ueber die ganze Welt . Numerous maps were drawn up in cooperation with the engraver  |Christoph_Weigel_the_Elder|Christoph_Weigel_The_Elder| , who also published  Siebmachers Wappenbuch .     Homann died in Nuremberg in 1724. He was succeeded by his son Johann Christoph . The company carried on upon his death as Homann heirs company, managed by Johann Michael Franz and Johann Georg Ebersberger. After subsequent changes in management the company folded in 1852.  The company was known as Homann Erben , Homanniani Heredes , or Heritiers de Homann abroad.
