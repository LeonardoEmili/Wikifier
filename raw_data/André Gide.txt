        André Paul Guillaume Gide  was a French author and winner of the  |Nobel_Prize_in_Literature|Nobel_Prize_In_Literature|  . Gides career ranged from its beginnings in the  |symbolist|Symbolism|  movement, to the advent of  |anticolonialism|Anti-Imperialism|  between the two World Wars. The author of more than fifty books, at the time of his death his obituary in  The New York Times  described him as Frances greatest contemporary man of letters and judged the greatest French writer of this century by the literary cognoscenti.      Known for his fiction as well as his autobiographical works, Gide exposes to public view the conflict and eventual reconciliation of the two sides of his personality , which a strict and moralistic education had helped set at odds. Gides work can be seen as an investigation of freedom and empowerment in the face of moralistic and  |puritan|Puritan| ical constraints, and centers on his continuous effort to achieve intellectual honesty. His self-exploratory texts reflect his search of how to be fully oneself, including owning ones sexual nature, without at the same time betraying ones values. His political activity is shaped by the same ethos, as indicated by his repudiation of  |communism|Communism|  after his 1936 voyage to the  |USSR|Soviet_Union| .          Gide was born in Paris on 22 November 1869, into a middle-class  |Protestant|Protestant|  family. His father was a  |Paris_University|University_Of_Paris|  professor of law who died in 1880, Jean Paul Guillaume Gide, and his mother was Juliette Maria Rondeaux. His uncle was the political economist  |Charles_Gide|Charles_Gide| .
