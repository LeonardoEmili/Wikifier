        In  |medicine|Medicine|  and  |biology|Biology| , scatology or coprology is the study of  |feces|Feces| .     Scatological studies allow one to determine a wide range of biological information about a creature, including its  |diet|Diet|  ,  |health|Health|  and  |diseases|Disease|  such as  |tapeworms|Tapeworms| . The word derives from the  |Greek|Greek_Language|    meaning dung, feces ; coprology derives from the Greek  of similar meaning. , .       A comprehensive study of scatology was documented by  |John_Gregory_Bourke|John_Gregory_Bourke|  under the title  Rites of All Nations . An abbreviated version of the work , was published as The Portable Scatalog in 1994.        In  |psychology|Psychology| , a scatology is an obsession with  |excretion|Excretion|  or  |excrement|Excrement| , or the study of such obsessions.       In  |sexual_fetishism|Sexual_Fetishism| , scatology  refers to  |coprophilia|Coprophilia| , when a person is  |sexually_aroused|Sexual_Arousal|  by fecal matter, whether in the use of feces in various sexual acts, watching someone  |defecating|Defecation| , or simply seeing the feces. Entire subcultures in sexuality are devoted to this fetish.       In  |literature|Literature| , scatological is a term to denote the literary  |trope|Trope|  of the  |grotesque_body|Grotesque_Body| . It is used to describe works that make particular reference to excretion or excrement, as well as to  |toilet_humor|Toilet_Humour| . Well-known for his scatological tropes is the late medieval fictional character of  |Till_Eulenspiegel|Till_Eulenspiegel| . Another common example is  |John_Drydens|John_Dryden|   Mac Flecknoe , a poem that employs extensive scatological imagery to ridicule Drydens contemporary  |Thomas_Shadwell|Thomas_Shadwell| . In German literature in particular is a wealth of scatological texts and references, which includes such books as  |Collofinos|Collofino|  Non Olet.  A case which has provoked an unusual amount of comment in the academic literature is  |Mozarts_scatological_humour|Mozart_And_Scatology| . Smith, in his review of English literatures representations of scatology from the Middle Ages to the 18th century, notes two attitudes towards scatology. One of these emphasises the merry and the carnivalesque. This is found in  |Chaucer|Geoffrey_Chaucer|  and  |Shakespeare|William_Shakespeare| . The other attitude is one of self-disgust and misanthropy. This is found in the works of the  |Earl_of_Rochester|John_Wilmot_2Nd_Earl_Of_Rochester|  and  |Jonathan_Swift|Jonathan_Swift| .
