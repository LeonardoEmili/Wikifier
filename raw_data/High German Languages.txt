As a technical term, the high in High German is a geographical reference to the group of dialects that forms High German , out of which developed  |Standard_German|Standard_German| ,  |Yiddish|Yiddish|  and  |Luxembourgish|Luxembourgish| . It refers to the  |Central_Uplands|Central_Uplands|   and  |Alpine|Alps|  areas of central and southern Germany; it also includes Luxembourg, Austria, Liechtenstein and most of Switzerland. This is opposed to  |Low_German|Low_German| , which is spoken in the lowlands and along the flat sea coasts of the  |North_German_Plain|North_German_Plain| . See the definition of high in the Oxford English Dictionary : ... situated far above ground, sealevel, etc; upper, inland, as ... High German . Divisions between subfamilies within Germanic are rarely precisely defined, because most form continuous clines, with adjacent  |dialects|Dialect|  being mutually intelligible and more separated ones not. In particular, there has never been an original  |Proto-High_German|Proto-Language|  . For this and other reasons, the idea of representing the relationships between  |West_Germanic|West_Germanic_Languages|  language forms in a tree diagram at all is controversial among linguists.
