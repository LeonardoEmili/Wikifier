        Stanley Allison Rogers was a Canadian  |folk_music|Folk_Music| ian and  |songwriter|Songwriter| .     Rogers was noted for his rich,  |baritone|Baritone|  voice and his traditional-sounding songs which were frequently inspired by  |Canadian_history|Canadian_History|  and the daily lives of working people, especially those from the fishing villages of the  |Maritime_provinces|Maritime_Provinces|  and, later, the farms of the  |Canadian_prairies|Canadian_Prairies|  and  |Great_Lakes|Great_Lakes| . Rogers died in a fire aboard  |Air_Canada_Flight_797|Air_Canada_Flight_797|  on the ground at the  |Greater_Cincinnati_Airport|Greater_Cincinnati_Airport|  at the age of 33.       Rogers was born in  |Hamilton,_Ontario|Hamilton,_Ontario|   the eldest son of Nathan Allison Rogers and Valerie  Rogers, two  |Maritimers|Maritimers|  who had relocated to  |Ontario|Ontario|  in search of work shortly after their marriage in July 1948. Although Rogers was raised in  |Binbrook,_Ontario|Binbrook,_Ontario| ,  he often spent summers visiting family in  |Guysborough_County,_Nova_Scotia|Guysborough_County,_Nova_Scotia| .       It was there that he became familiar with the way of life in the  |Maritimes|Maritimes| , an influence which was to have a profound impact on his subsequent musical development. He was interested in music from an early age, reportedly beginning to sing shortly after learning to speak. He received his first guitar, a miniature hand-built by his uncle Lee Bushell, when he was five years of age.  He was exposed to a variety of music influences, but among the most lasting were the  |country_and_western|Country_And_Western|  tunes his uncles would sing during family get-togethers. Throughout his childhood, he would practice his singing and playing along with his brother  |Garnet|Garnet_Rogers| , six years his junior.     While Rogers was attending  |Saltfleet_High_School,_Stoney_Creek,_Ontario|Saltfleet_High_School| ,  he started to meet other young people interested in  |folk_music|Folk_Music| , although at this time he was dabbling in  |rock_and_roll|Rock_And_Roll| , singing and playing  |bass_guitar|Bass_Guitar|  in  |garage_bands|Garage_Bands|  such as Stanley and the Living Stones and The Hobbits .  After high school, Rogers briefly attended both  |McMaster_University|Mcmaster_University|  and  |Trent_University|Trent_University| , where he performed in small venues with other student musicians, including  |Ian_Tamblyn|Ian_Tamblyn| ,  |Chris_Ward|Chris_Ward|  and fellow Hobbit Nigel Russell. . In 1973, Rogers recorded three singles for Polygram: Three Pennies , Guysborough Train , and Past Fifty.      In 1976, Rogers recorded his debut album, Fogartys Cove, released in 1977 on Barnswallow Records.  The albums subject matter dealt almost entirely with life in  |maritime_Canada|Maritimes| , and was an immediate success. Rogers then formed  |Fogartys_Cove_Music|Fogartys_Cove_Music| , and bought Barnswallow during the production of Turnaround, allowing him to release his own albums.  |Posthumously|Posthumous_Work| , additional albums were released.       Rogers songs often had a  |Celtic|Celtic_Music|  feel which was due, in part, to his frequent use of  |DADGAD|Dadgad|  guitar tuning. He regularly used his  |William_Grit_Laskin|William_Laskin|  built  |12-string_guitar|12-String_Guitar|  in his performances. His best-known pieces include  |Northwest_Passage|Northwest_Passage|  ,  |Barretts_Privateers|Barretts_Privateers|  ,  |The_Mary_Ellen_Carter|The_Mary_Ellen_Carter|  ,  |Make_and_Break_Harbour|Make_And_Break_Harbour|  ,  |The_Idiot|The_Idiot|  ,  |Fogartys_Cove|Fogartys_Cove|  , and  |White_Squall|White_Squall|  .       Rogers died alongside 22 other passengers most likely of  |smoke_inhalation|Smoke_Inhalation|  on June 2, 1983, while traveling on  |Air_Canada_Flight_797|Air_Canada_Flight_797|   after performing at the  |Kerrville_Folk_Festival|Kerrville_Folk_Festival| . The airliner was flying from  |Dallas|Dallas| ,  |Texas|Texas| , to  |Toronto|Toronto|  and  |Montreal|Montreal|  when a fire of unknown ignition source within the vanity or toilet shroud of the aft washroom forced it to make an  |emergency_landing|Emergency_Landing|  at the  |Greater_Cincinnati_Airport|Greater_Cincinnati_Airport|  in northern Kentucky.     There were initially no visible flames, and after attempts to extinguish the fire were unsuccessful, smoke filled the cabin. Upon landing, the planes doors were opened, allowing the five crew and 18 of the passengers to escape, but approximately 60 to 90 seconds into the evacuation the oxygen rushing in from outside caused a flash fire.  Rogers was one of the passengers still on the plane at the time of the fire.     His ashes were scattered in the  |Atlantic_Ocean|Atlantic_Ocean|  off the coast of Nova Scotia.       Rogers legacy includes his recordings, songbook, and plays for which he was commissioned to write music. His songs are still frequently covered by other musicians, and are perennial favourites at Canadian  |campfires|Campfires|  and  |song_circles|Song_Circles| . Members of Rogers band, including his brother  |Garnet_Rogers|Garnet_Rogers| , continue to be active performers and form a significant part of the fabric of contemporary Canadian folk music. Following his death, he was nominated for the  |1984_Juno_Awards|Juno_Awards_Of_1984|  in the category for  |Best_Male_Vocalist|Juno_Award_For_Artist_Of_The_Year| . That same year, he was posthumously awarded the Diplôme d’Honneur of the  |Canadian_Conference_of_the_Arts|Canadian_Conference_Of_The_Arts| .  In 1994, his posthumous live album  Home in Halifax  was likewise nominated for  |Best_Roots_and_Traditional_Album|Juno_Award_For_Roots_&_Traditional_Album_Of_The_Year_–_Solo| .     His widow, Ariel, continues to oversee his estate and legacy. His music and lyrics have been featured in numerous written publications and films. For instance, his lyrics have appeared in school poetry books, taking their place alongside acknowledged classics. His song  |Northwest_Passage|Northwest_Passage|  was featured in the last episode of the TV show  Due South , his songs  |Barretts_Privateers|Barretts_Privateers|  and  |Watching_the_Apples_Grow|Watching_The_Apples_Grow|  having been previously featured. Barretts Privateers has also been used extensively in promotion ads for  |Alexander_Keiths|Alexander_Keiths_Brewery|  ale. In the 2005  |CTV|Ctv_Television_Network| |made-for-TV_movie|Made-For-Tv_Movie|  on the life of  |Terry_Fox|Terry_Fox| , Rogers  |Turnaround|Turnaround|  is the music over the closing shot. As the movie ends, Fox is depicted, alone, striding up a hill, while the lyric And yours was the open road. Many of his songs on the albums  Northwest Passage  and  From Fresh Water  refer to events in Canadian history.      |Adrienne_Clarkson|Adrienne_Clarkson| , who, prior to serving as the  |Governor_General_of_Canada|Governor_General_Of_Canada|  from 1999 to 2005, had worked for the  |Canadian_Broadcasting_Corporation|Canadian_Broadcasting_Corporation| , highlighted Rogers career in a 1989  |television_documentary|Television_Documentary|  called  One Warm Line  on  |CBC_Television|Cbc_Television| ; she also quoted Rogers in her investitural address.     When CBCs  |Peter_Gzowski|Peter_Gzowski|  asked Canadians to pick an alternate national anthem, Northwest Passage was the overwhelming choice.       The  |Stan_Rogers_Folk_Festival|Stan_Rogers_Folk_Festival|  is held every year in  |Canso,_Nova_Scotia|Canso,_Nova_Scotia| . In 1995, several artists performed two nights of concerts at Halifaxs  |Rebecca_Cohn_Auditorium|Rebecca_Cohn_Auditorium| , which were released on album that year as  Remembering Stan Rogers .     Rogers is also a lasting fixture of the Canadian folk festival  |Summerfolk|Summerfolk_Music_And_Crafts_Festival| , held annually in  |Owen_Sound,_Ontario|Owen_Sound,_Ontario| , where the main stage and amphitheater are dedicated as the Stan Rogers Memorial Canopy . The festival is firmly fixed in tradition, with Rogers song  |The_Mary_Ellen_Carter|The_Mary_Ellen_Carter|  being sung by all involved, including the audience and a medley of acts at the festival.     At  |The_Canmore_Folk_Festival|Canmore_Folk_Music_Festival| , Albertas longest running folk music festival, performers take to the Stan Rogers Memorial Stage, which is the festivals main stage.     Stans son,  |Nathan_Rogers|Nathan_Rogers| , is also an established Canadian folk artist with a voice and lyrical acumen similar to his fathers. He has released two critically acclaimed solo discs and tours internationally as a solo act and in the trio Dry Bones.     In 2007, Rogers was recognized posthumously with a National Achievement Award at the annual  |SOCAN|Socan|  Awards held in Toronto.
