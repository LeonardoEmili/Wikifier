The politics of the Cook Islands, an  |associated_state|Associated_State| , takes place in a framework of a  |parliamentary|Parliamentary_System| |representative_democracy|Representative_Democracy|  within a  |constitutional_monarchy|Constitutional_Monarchy| . The  |Queen_of_New_Zealand|Monarchy_In_New_Zealand| , represented in the Cook Islands by the  |Queens_Representative|Queens_Representative| , is the  |Head_of_State|Head_Of_State| ; the prime minister is the  |head_of_government|Head_Of_Government|  and of a  |multi-party_system|Multi-Party_System| . The Islands are self-governing in free association with  |New_Zealand|New_Zealand|  and are fully responsible for internal affairs. New Zealand retains some responsibility for external affairs, in consultation with the Cook Islands. In recent years, the Cook Islands have taken on more of its own external affairs; as of 2005, it has diplomatic relations in its own name with eighteen other countries.  |Executive_power|Executive_Power|  is exercised by the government, while  |legislative_power|Legislative_Power|  is vested in both the government and  |the_islands_parliament|Parliament_Of_The_Cook_Islands| . The  |judiciary|Judiciary|  is independent of the executive and the legislatures.       The  |Constitution_of_the_Cook_Islands|Constitution_Of_The_Cook_Islands|  took effect on August 4, 1965, when the Cook Islands became a  |self-governing|Self-Government|  territory in  |free_association|Associated_State|  with New Zealand.
