|_Field_Marshal|Field_Marshal|  Harold Rupert Leofric George Alexander, 1st Earl Alexander of Tunis,  , was a senior  |British_Army|British_Army|  officer who served with distinction in both the  |First_World_War|First_World_War|  and the  |Second_World_War|Second_World_War|  and, afterwards, as  |Governor_General_of_Canada|Governor_General_Of_Canada| , the  |17th|List_Of_Governors_General_Of_Canadagovernors_General_Of_Canada_1867–Present|  since  |Canadian_Confederation|Canadian_Confederation| .     Alexander was born in  |London|London| , England, to  |aristocratic|Aristocracy|  parents and was educated at  |Harrow|Harrow_School|  before moving on to the  |Royal_Military_College_Sandhurst|Royal_Military_College_Sandhurst| , for training as an army officer of the  |Irish_Guards|Irish_Guards| . He rose to prominence through his service in the First World War, receiving numerous honours and decorations, and continued his military career through various British campaigns across Europe and Asia. In World War II, Alexander oversaw the final stages of the  |Allied|Allies_Of_World_War_Ii| |_evacuation_from_Dunkirk|Dunkirk_Evacuation|  and subsequently held high-ranking field commands in  |Burma|Burma_Campaign| ,  |North_Africa|Tunisia_Campaign|  and  |Italy|Italian_Campaign| , including serving as Commander-in-Chief Middle East and commanding the  |18th_Army_Group|18Th_Army_Group|  in  |Tunisia|Tunisia| . He then commanded the  |15th_Army_Group|15Th_Army_Group|  for the  |capture_of_Sicily|Allied_Invasion_Of_Sicily|  and again in Italy before receiving his field marshals baton and being made Supreme Allied Commander Mediterranean.     In 1946 he was appointed as governor general by  |King|Monarchy_Of_Canada| |_George_VI|George_Vi| , on the recommendation of  |Prime_Minister_of_Canada|Prime_Minister_Of_Canada| |_William_Lyon_Mackenzie_King|William_Lyon_Mackenzie_King| , to replace  |the_Earl_of_Athlone|Alexander_Cambridge_1St_Earl_Of_Athlone|  as  |viceroy|Viceroy| , and he occupied the post until succeeded by  |Vincent_Massey|Vincent_Massey|  in 1952. Alexander proved to be enthusiastic about the Canadian wilderness and was a popular governor general with Canadians. He was the last non-Canadian-born governor general before the appointment of  |Adrienne_Clarkson|Adrienne_Clarkson|  in 1999, as well as the last governor general to be a peer.     After the end of his viceregal tenure, Alexander was sworn into the  |Queens_Privy_Council_for_Canada|Queens_Privy_Council_For_Canada|  and thereafter,  in order to serve as the  |British_Minister_of_Defence|Minister_Of_Defence|  in the  |Cabinet|Cabinet_Of_The_United_Kingdom|  of  |Winston_Churchill|Winston_Churchill| , into the Imperial  |Privy_Council|Her_Majestys_Most_Honourable_Privy_Council| . Alexander retired in 1954 and died in 1969.       Alexander was born in London into an aristocratic family from  |County_Tyrone|County_Tyrone|  of  |Ulster_Scots|Ulster_Scots_People|  descent. He was the third son of  |James_Alexander_4th_Earl_of_Caledon|James_Alexander_4Th_Earl_Of_Caledon| , and the Countess of Caledon, a daughter of  |the_3rd_Earl_of_Norbury|Earl_Of_Norbury| . Alexander was educated at  |Hawtreys|Hawtreys|  and  |Harrow_School|Harrow_School| , there participating as the 11th batsman in the sensational  |Fowlers_Match|Fowlers_Match|  against  |Eton_College|Eton_College|  in 1910.  Though Alexander toyed with the notion of becoming an artist, he went instead on to the  |Royal_Military_College_Sandhurst|Royal_Military_College_Sandhurst| . He was promoted to  |lieutenant|Lieutenant|  in December 1912.        Alexander spent most of the  |First_World_War|First_World_War|  on the  |Western_Front|Western_Front| . As a 22-year-old  |platoon|Platoon|  commander in the 1st Battalion, Irish Guards, he served with the  |British_Expeditionary_Force|British_Expeditionary_Force|  in 1914. He took part in the  |retreat_from_Mons|Battle_Of_Mons|  and was wounded at  |First_Ypres|First_Battle_Of_Ypres|  and invalided home.  He was promoted to temporary  |captain|Captain|  on 15 November 1914 and permanent captain in the newly raised 2nd Battalion on 7 February the following year.      Alexander returned to the Western Front in August 1915, fought at the  |Battle_of_Loos|Battle_Of_Loos|  and was, for ten days in October 1915, an  |acting|Acting| |_major|Major|  and acting  |Commanding_Officer|Commanding_Officer|  of the 1st Battalion, Irish Guards as a Battle Casualty Replacement . He then returned to the 2nd Battalion as a company officer and, in January 1916, received the  |Military_Cross|Military_Cross|  for his bravery at Loos.  For service in the  |Battle_of_the_Somme|Battle_Of_The_Somme|  on  |15_September_1916|Battle_Of_Flers_Courcelette| , he was, in October, appointed to the  |Distinguished_Service_Order|Distinguished_Service_Order|  ,  the citation for which read: For conspicuous gallantry in action. He was the life and soul of the attack, and throughout the day led forward not only his own men but men of all regiments. He held the trenches gained in spite of heavy machine gun fire. In the same month, Alexander was further honoured with induction into the French  |Légion_dhonneur|Légion_Dhonneur| .
