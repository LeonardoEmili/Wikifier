      Merseburg  is a  |town|Town|  in the south of the  |German|Germany|  state of  |Saxony-Anhalt|Saxony-Anhalt|  on the river  |Saale|Saale| , approx. 14  km south of  |Halle|Halle|  and 30 km west of  |Leipzig|Leipzig| . It is the capital of the  |Saalekreis|Saalekreis|  district. It had a  |diocese|Diocese|  founded by Archbishop  |Adalbert_of_Magdeburg|Adalbert_Of_Magdeburg| .   The  |University_of_Merseburg|University_Of_Merseburg|  is located within the town. Merseburg has around 33,000 inhabitants. Merseburg is part of the  |Central_German_Metropolitan_Region|Central_German_Metropolitan_Region| .                     Venenien was incorporated into Merseburg on 1 January 1949. The parish Kötzschen followed on 1 July 1950. Since 30 May 1994, Meuschau is part of Merseburg. Beuna was annexed on 1 January 2009.   |Geusa|Geusa|  is a part of Merseburg since 1 January 2010.               Merseburg was first mentioned in 850. King  |Henry_the_Fowler|Henry_The_Fowler|  built a royal palace at Merseburg; in the 933  |Battle_of_Riade|Battle_Of_Riade| , he gained his great victory over the  |Hungarians|Hungarians|  in the vicinity.      |Thietmar|Dětmar| , appointed in 973, became the first bishop of the newly created  |bishopric_of_Prague|Bishopric_Of_Prague|  in  |Bohemia|Bohemia| . Prague had been part of the  |archbishopric_of_Mainz|Archbishopric_Of_Mainz|  for a hundred years before that. From 968 until the  |Protestant_Reformation|Protestant_Reformation| , Merseburg was the seat of the  |Bishop_of_Merseburg|Bishop_Of_Merseburg| , and in addition to being for a time the residence of the  |margraves_of_Meissen|Margraviate_Of_Meissen| , it was a favorite residence of the German kings during the 10th, 11th and 12th centuries. Fifteen  |diets|Reichstag|  were held here during the Middle Ages, during which time its fairs enjoyed the importance which was afterwards transferred to those of  |Leipzig|Leipzig| . Merseburg was the site of a failed assassination attempt on Polish ruler  |Bolesław_I_Chrobry|Bolesław_I_Chrobry|  in 1002.  The town suffered severely during the  |German_Peasants_War|German_Peasants_War|  and also during the  |Thirty_Years_War|Thirty_Years_War| .          From 1657 to 1738 Merseburg was the residence of the Dukes of  |Saxe-Merseburg|Duchy_Of_Saxe-Merseburg| , after which it fell to the  |Electorate_of_Saxony|Electorate_Of_Saxony| . In 1815 following the  |Napoleonic_Wars|Napoleonic_Wars| , the town became part of the  |Prussia|Prussia| n  |Province_of_Saxony|Province_Of_Saxony| .     Merseburg is where the  |Merseburg_Incantations|Merseburg_Incantations|  were rediscovered in 1841. Written down in  |Old_High_German|Old_High_German| , they are hitherto the only preserved German documents with a heathen theme. One of them is a charm to release warriors caught during battle, and the other is a charm to heal a horses sprained foot.     At the beginning of the 20th century, Merseburg was transformed into an industrial town, largely due to the pioneering work done by  |Carl_Bosch|Carl_Bosch|  and  |Friedrich_Bergius|Friedrich_Bergius| , who laid down the scientific fundamentals of the catalytic high-pressure  |ammonia|Ammonia|  synthesis from 1909 to 1913. Enterprises, too, blazed a trail in the course of the transformational process. Ultimately, a chemical park emerged at nearby  |Leuna|Leuna|  which is one of the most modern sites of its kind in Europe with high ecological standards.     Merseburg was badly damaged in  |World_War_II|World_War_Ii| . In 23 air raids 6,200 dwellings were completely or partly destroyed. Eckardt Götz Schicksale deutscher Baudenkmale im zweiten Weltkrieg, Band 2, p. 332, Henschelverlag, Berlin The historic town centre was almost completely destroyed.     Briefly part of  |Saxony-Anhalt|Saxony-Anhalt|  after the war, it was then administered within the  Bezirk   |Halle|Halle,_Saxony-Anhalt|  in  |East_Germany|East_Germany| . It became part of Saxony-Anhalt again after  |reunification_of_Germany|Reunification_Of_Germany| .       Like many towns in the former East Germany, Merseburg has had a general decline in population since German Reunification despite annexing and merging with a number of smaller nearby villages.
