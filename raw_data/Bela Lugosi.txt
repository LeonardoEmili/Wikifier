           Béla Ferenc Dezső Blaskó , known professionally as Bela Lugosi , was a  |Hungarian-American|Hungarian-American|  actor best remembered for portraying  |Count_Dracula|Count_Dracula|  in the  |1931_film|Dracula|  and for his roles in other  |horror_films|Horror_Films| .     After playing small parts on the stage in his native Hungary, Lugosi gained his first role in a film in 1917. He had to leave the country after  |the_failed_Hungarian_Communist_Revolution_of_1919|Hungarian_Soviet_Republic|  because of his socialist activism. He acted in several films in  |Weimar_Germany|Weimar_Germany|  before arriving in the United States as a seaman on a merchant ship.     In 1927, he appeared as Count Dracula in a Broadway adaptation of  |Bram_Stokers|Bram_Stoker| |novel|Dracula| . He later appeared in the 1931 film Dracula directed by  |Tod_Browning|Tod_Browning|  and produced by  |Universal_Pictures|Universal_Studios| . Through the 1930s, he occupied an important niche in horror films, with their East European setting, but his Hungarian accent limited his potential casting, and he unsuccessfully tried to avoid typecasting.     Meanwhile, he was often paired with  |Boris_Karloff|Boris_Karloff| , who was able to demand top billing. To his frustration, Lugosi, a charter member of the American  |Screen_Actors_Guild|Screen_Actors_Guild| , was increasingly restricted to minor parts, kept employed by the studio principally so that they could put his name on the posters. Among his pairings with Karloff, he performed major roles only in  The Black Cat  ,  The Raven  , and  Son of Frankenstein  ; even in The Raven, Karloff received top billing despite Lugosi performing the lead role. By this time, Lugosi had been receiving regular medication for  |sciatic_neuritis|Sciatica| , and he became addicted to  |morphine|Morphine|  and  |methadone|Methadone| . This drug dependence was known to producers, and the offers eventually dwindled to a few parts in  |Ed_Woods|Ed_Wood|  low-budget films—including a brief appearance in  Plan 9 from Outer Space  . Lugosi, who was married five times and had one son,  |Bela_George|Bela_G._Lugosi| , died of a heart attack on August 16, 1956. Dracula         Lugosi was approached in the summer of 1927 to star in a  |Broadway_theatre|Broadway_Theatre|  production of  Dracula , which had been adapted by  |Hamilton_Deane|Hamilton_Deane|  and  |John_L._Balderston|John_L._Balderston|  from  |Bram_Stokers|Bram_Stoker|  1897  |novel|Dracula| .  The  |Horace_Liveright|Horace_Liveright|  production was successful, running for 261 performances before touring the United States to much fanfare and critical acclaim throughout 1928 and 1929. In 1928, Lugosi decided to stay in California when the play ended its West Coast run. His performance had piqued the interest of  |Fox_Film|Fox_Film| , and he was cast in the studios silent film  The Veiled Woman  . He also appeared in the film  Prisoners  , believed  |lost|Lost_Film| , which was released in both silent and  |talkie|Sound_Film|  versions.             In 1929, with no other film roles in sight, he returned to the stage as Dracula for a short West Coast tour of the play. Lugosi remained in California where he resumed his film work under contract with Fox, appearing in early talkies often as a  |heavy|Villain|  or an exotic  |sheik|Sheikh|  . He also continued to lobby for his prized role in the film version of  Dracula .      Despite his critically acclaimed performance on stage, Lugosi was not  |Universal_Pictures|Universal_Studios|  first choice for the role of Dracula when the company optioned the rights to the Deane play and began production in 1930. Different prominent actors were considered before Browning cast Lugosi for the role, but the film was a hit.             Through his association with  Dracula  , Lugosi found himself  |typecast|Typecasting|  as a  |horror|Horror_Film|  villain in films such as  Murders in the Rue Morgue  ,  The Raven  , and  Son of Frankenstein  for Universal, and the independent  White Zombie  . His accent, while a part of his image, limited the roles he could play.     Lugosi did attempt to break type by auditioning for other roles.
