         Eli Herschel Wallach  was an American film, television and stage actor whose career spanned more than six decades, beginning in the late 1940s. Trained in stage acting, which he enjoyed doing most, he became one of the greatest  |character_actors|Character_Actor|  ever to appear on stage and screen , with over 90 film credits. On stage, he often co-starred with his wife,  |Anne_Jackson|Anne_Jackson| , becoming one of the best-known acting couples in the American theater. As a stage and screen character actor, Wallach had one of the longest-ever careers in show business, spanning 62 years from his Broadway debut to his last two major Hollywood studio movies .     Wallach initially studied  |method_acting|Method_Acting|  under  |Sanford_Meisner|Sanford_Meisner| , and later became a founding member of the  |Actors_Studio|Actors_Studio| , where he studied under  |Lee_Strasberg|Lee_Strasberg| . His versatility gave him the ability to play a wide variety of different roles throughout his career, primarily as a  |supporting_actor|Supporting_Actor| .     For his debut screen performance in  Baby Doll  , he won a  |BAFTA_Award_for_Best_Newcomer|Bafta_Award_For_Most_Promising_Newcomer_To_Leading_Film_Roles|  and a  |Golden_Globe_Award|Golden_Globe_Award|  nomination. Among his other most famous roles are Calvera in  The Magnificent Seven  , Guido in  The Misfits  , and Tuco in  The Good, the Bad and the Ugly  Other notable portrayals include outlaw Charlie Gant in  How the West Was Won  , Hitman Leon B. Little in  Tough Guys  ,  |Don_Altobello|Don_Altobello|  in  The Godfather Part III , Cotton Weinberger in  The Two Jakes  , and Arthur Abbott in  The Holiday  . One of Americas most prolific screen actors, Wallach remained active well into his nineties, with roles as recently as 2010 in  Wall Street: Money Never Sleeps  and  The Ghost Writer .     In 1988, Eli Wallach was inducted into the  |American_Theater_Hall_of_Fame|American_Theater_Hall_Of_Fame| .      Wallach has also received  |BAFTA_Awards|British_Academy_Film_Awards| ,  |Tony_Awards|Tony_Award|  and  |Emmy_Awards|Emmy_Award|  for his work, and received an  |Academy_Honorary_Award|Academy_Honorary_Award|  at the second annual  |Governors_Awards|Governors_Awards| , presented on November 13, 2010.     In March 2017, the  |Harry_Ransom_Center|Harry_Ransom_Center|  announced the acquisition of Wallachs papers, along with those of his wife. It will be made public when cataloging is complete. Despite the fact that he eventually acted in over 90 films and almost as many television dramas, he continued to accept stage parts throughout his career, often with Jackson. A pivotal moment in Wallachs career came in 1953, when he, along with Frank Sinatra and Harvey Lembeck , tried out for the role of Maggio in the film  |From_Here_to_Eternity|From_Here_To_Eternity| . Sinatra biographer Kitty Kelly notes that while Sinatras test was good, it had none of the consummate acting ability of Wallach. Producer Harry Cohn and director Fred Zinnemann were dazzled by Wallachs screen test and wanted him to play the part. However, Wallach had previously been offered an important role in another Tennessee Williams play,  |Camino_Real|Camino_Real| , to be directed by Elia Kazan , and turned down the movie role. Wallach said that when he learned that the play had finally received financing, he grabbed the opportunity: It was a remarkable piece of writing by the leading playwright in America and it was going to be directed by the countrys best. There really wasnt much of a choice for me. Kelly, Kitty. His Way: An Unauthorized Biography Of Frank Sinatra, Random House . The film, however, went on to win eight Academy Awards, including one for Sinatra, which revived his career. Wallach recalled afterwards, Whenever Sinatra saw me, he’d say, Hello, you crazy actor! Wallach, however, claimed to have no regrets.
