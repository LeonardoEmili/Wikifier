              Hera  is the goddess of women, marriage, family, and childbirth in  |ancient_Greek_religion|Ancient_Greek_Religion|  and  |myth|Greek_Mythology| , one of the  |Twelve_Olympians|Twelve_Olympians|  and the sister-wife of  |Zeus|Zeus| . She is the daughter of the  |Titans|Titan| |Cronus|Cronus|  and  |Rhea|Rhea| . Hera rules over  |Mount_Olympus|Mount_Olympus|  as queen of the gods. A matronly figure, Hera served as both the patroness and protectress of married women, presiding over weddings and blessing marital unions. One of Heras defining characteristics is her jealous and vengeful nature against Zeus numerous lovers and illegitimate offspring, as well as the mortals who cross her.     Hera is commonly seen with the animals she considers sacred including the  |cow|Cattle| ,  |lion|Lion|  and the  |peacock|Peafowl| . Portrayed as majestic and solemn, often enthroned, and crowned with the  polos  , Hera may hold a pomegranate in her hand, emblem of fertile blood and death and a substitute for the narcotic capsule of the  |opium|Opium|  poppy. Heras importance in the early archaic period is attested by the large building projects undertaken in her honor. The temples of Hera in the two main centers of her  |cult|Cult| , the  |Heraion_of_Samos|Heraion_Of_Samos|  and the  |Heraion_of_Argos|Heraion_Of_Argos|  in the  |Argolis|Argolis| , were the very earliest monumental  |Greek_temples|Greek_Temple|  constructed, in the 8th century BCE.
