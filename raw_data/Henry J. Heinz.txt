              Henry John Heinz was a  |German_American|German_Americans|  entrepreneur who founded the  |H_J_Heinz_Company|H_J_Heinz_Company|  based in  |Pittsburgh|Pittsburgh| ,  |Pennsylvania|Pennsylvania| . He was born in that city, the son of German immigrants who came independently to the United States in the early 1840s. Heinz developed his business into a national company which made more than 60 food products; one of its first was  |tomato_ketchup|Tomato_Ketchup| . He was influential for introducing high sanitary standards for food manufacturing. He also exercised a paternal relationship with his workers, providing health benefits, recreation facilities, and cultural amenities. Heinz was the great-grandfather of former  |U_S_Senator|United_States_Senate| |_H_John_Heinz_III|H_John_Heinz_Iii|  of  |Pennsylvania|Pennsylvania|  and a second cousin of  |Frederick_Trump|Frederick_Trump| , and second cousin twice removed of  |Donald_J_Trump|Donald_J_Trump|        Henry John Heinz was born in Pittsburgh on October 11, 1844, the son of German immigrants John Henry Heinz , of  |Kallstadt|Kallstadt| ,  |Palatinate|Palatinate| ,  |Kingdom_of_Bavaria|Kingdom_Of_Bavaria| , and Anna Margaretha Schmidt , of Kruspis,  |Haunetal|Haunetal| ,  |Hesse_Kassel|Electorate_Of_Hesse| .  His father immigrated to the United States at age 29 in 1840, his mother at age 21 in 1843. They were married December 4, 1843, in  |Birmingham_Pennsylvania|South_Side| , on the south side of Pittsburgh, where they first met. Anna Schmidt was the daughter of a Lutheran minister; John Heinz was also Lutheran.      Heinz was raised and confirmed as a  |Lutheran|Lutheran| . Later in life he also worshipped as a member of  |Methodist|Methodist|  and  |Presbyterian|Presbyterian|  churches, and worked closely with  |Baptists|Baptists|  as well.     Through his mothers family, Henry Heinz was a second cousin to  |Frederick_Trump|Frederick_Trump| , who emigrated to the United States in 1885.   Trump was the immigrant ancestor and paternal grandfather of  |Donald_Trump|Donald_Trump|  of New York City.        Henry John Heinz began packing foodstuffs on a small scale at  |Sharpsburg_Pennsylvania|Sharpsburg_Pennsylvania| , in 1869. There he founded Heinz Noble & Company with a friend, L. Clarence Noble, and started marketing packaged  |horseradish|Horseradish| . The company went bankrupt in 1875. The following year Heinz founded another company, F & J Heinz, with his brother John Heinz and a cousin, Frederick Heinz. One of this companys first products was  |tomato_ketchup|Tomato_Ketchup| .     The company continued to grow, and in 1888 Heinz bought out his other two partners and reorganized as the  |H_J_Heinz_Company|H_J_Heinz_Company| , the name carried to the present day. The companys slogan,  |57_varieties|Heinz_57| , was introduced by Heinz in 1896; by then the company was selling more than 60 different products.  Heinz said he chose 5 because it was his  |lucky_number|Lucky_Number|  and the number 7 was his wifes lucky number.      H. J. Heinz Company was incorporated in 1905, and Heinz served as its first president, leading in the position for the rest of his life. Under his tutelage, the company was noted for fair treatment of workers and for pioneering safe and sanitary food preparation. He provided his employees with free medical care; recreation facilities such as gyms, swimming pools, and gardens; and educational opportunities such as libraries, free concerts, and lectures. Heinz led a successful lobbying effort in favor of the  |Pure_Food_and_Drug_Act|Pure_Food_And_Drug_Act|  in 1906. During  |World_War_I|World_War_I| , he worked with the  |Food_Administration|United_States_Food_Administration| .      He was a director in many financial institutions, and was chairman of a committee to devise ways of protecting Pittsburgh from floods.     At the time of Heinzs death in Pittsburgh at the age of 74, the H. J. Heinz Company had more than 20 food processing plants, and owned seed farms and container factories. Heinz was the grandfather of  |H_J_Heinz_II|H_J_Heinz_Ii| , the great-grandfather of  |U_S_Senator|U_S_Senator| |_H_John_Heinz_III|H_John_Heinz_Iii|  of  |Pennsylvania|Pennsylvania| , and great-great grandfather of Henry John Heinz IV,  |André_Thierstein_Heinz|André_Heinz|  and Christopher Drake Heinz. Another relative is  |Teresa_Heinz_Kerry|Teresa_Heinz| , widow of H. John Heinz III, who is married to ex-senator and former  |United_States_Secretary_of_State|United_States_Secretary_Of_State| |_John_Kerry|John_Kerry| .         Heinz married Sarah Sloan Young Heinz on September 3, 1869. She was of  |Scots_Irish|Scotch_Irish_American|  ancestry and had grown up in the Presbyterian Church. They had five children:    Irene Edwilda Heinz-Given    Clarence Henry Heinz   Howard Covode Heinz   Robert Eugene Heinz   Clifford Sloan Heinz   They were raised as Presbyterians.     Heinz was a man of faith. When he visited  |England|England| , his tourist stops included the graves of religious leaders  |John_Bunyan|John_Bunyan| ,  |Isaac_Watts|Isaac_Watts| , and  |John_Wesley|John_Wesley| . He visited a chapel that Wesley founded, later writing that I felt I was upon holy ground.  At the beginning of his will Heinz wrote: I desire to set forth, at the very beginning of this Will, as the most important item in it, a confession of my faith in  |Jesus_Christ|Jesus_Christ|  as my Savior.      A bronze statue of Heinz by  |Emil_Fuchs|Emil_Fuchs|  was dedicated on October 11, 1924 at the Heinz Company building in Pittsburgh.        Heinz died at his home May 14, 1919, after contracting  |pneumonia|Pneumonia| . His funeral was at  |East_Liberty_Presbyterian_Church|Cathedral_Of_Hope| . He was buried at  |Homewood_Cemetery|Homewood_Cemetery|  in Pittsburgh, in the Heinz Family  |Mausoleum|Mausoleum| .
