   Politics of Slovakia  takes place in a framework of a  |parliamentary|Parliamentary_System| |representative_democratic|Representative_Democracy| |republic|Republic| , with a  |multi-party_system|Multi-Party_System| .  |Legislative_power|Legislative_Power|  is vested in the parliament and it can be exercised in some cases also by the government or directly by citizens.      |Executive_power|Executive_Power|  is exercised by the government led by the  |Prime_Minister|List_Of_Prime_Ministers_Of_Slovakia| . The  |Judiciary|Judiciary|  is independent of the executive and the legislature. The  |President|List_Of_Presidents_Of_Slovakia|  is the head of the state.        Before the  |1989_revolution|Velvet_Revolution| ,  |Czechoslovakia|Czechoslovakia|  was a socialist dictatorship ruled by the  |Communist_Party_of_Czechoslovakia|Communist_Party_Of_Czechoslovakia| , technically together with the coalition of the so-called  |National_Front|National_Front| . Before the free democratic elections could take place after the revolution, a  |transitional_government|Transitional_Government|  was created.   1989  |President_of_Czechoslovakia|President_Of_Czechoslovakia| |Gustáv_Husák|Gustáv_Husák|  sworn in the  |Government_of_National_Understanding|Government_Of_National_Understanding|   headed by  |Marián_Čalfa|Marián_Čalfa|  and he himself  |abdicated|Abdicated| . It consisted of 10 communists and 9 non-communists and its main goal was to prepare for democratic elections, to establish  |market_economy|Market_Economy|  in the country and to start preparing a new constitution.     On 8–9 June 1990, the  |Czechoslovakian_parliamentary_election_of_1990|Czechoslovakian_Parliamentary_Election,_1990|  took place. Čalfas second government was disbanded on 27 June 1990, when it was replaced by the  |Government_of_National_Sacrifice|Government_Of_National_Sacrifice|  , also headed by Marián Čalfa. On 5–6 June 1992, the last elections in Czechoslovakia, the  |Czechoslovakian_parliamentary_election_of_1992|Czechoslovakian_Parliamentary_Election,_1992|  took place. Čalfas third government was disbanded on 2 July 1992, when it was replaced by the  |Caretaker_Government_of_Jan_Stráský|Caretaker_Government_Of_Jan_Stráský|  , headed by  |Jan_Stráský|Jan_Stráský| . The  |caretaker_government|Caretaker_Government|  was disbanded on 31 December 1992 together with the  |dissolution|Dissolution_Of_Czechoslovakia|  of the  |Czech_and_Slovak_Federative_Republic|Czech_And_Slovak_Federative_Republic| .     Due to  |federalism|Federalism| , immediately after the  |1989_revolution|Velvet_Revolution| , two national governments  were created as well under the federal Czechoslovak government. In Slovakia it was headed by  |Milan_Čič|Milan_Čič|  and it was established on 12 December 1989 and disbanded on 26 June 1990. On 8–9 June 1990, the  |Slovak_parliamentary_election_of_1990|Slovak_Parliamentary_Election,_1990|  took place together with the federal Czechoslovak elections. Čičs government was followed by the First Government of  |Vladimír_Mečiar|Vladimír_Mečiar|  , Government of  |Ján_Čarnogurský|Ján_Čarnogurský|  and the Second Government of  |Vladimír_Mečiar|Vladimír_Mečiar|  . On 5–6 June the  |Slovak_parliamentary_election_of_1992|Slovak_Parliamentary_Election,_1992|  took place.       The  |Constitution_of_the_Slovak_Republic|Constitution_Of_The_Slovak_Republic|  was ratified 1 September 1992 and became effective 1 October 1992 . It was amended in September 1998 to allow direct election of the president and again in February 2001 due to EU admission requirements.   The civil law system is based on Austro-Hungarian codes. The legal code was modified to comply with the obligations of Organization on Security and Cooperation in Europe and to expunge the Marxist–Leninist legal theory.
